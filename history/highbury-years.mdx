## The Highbury Years — 1913 to 2006

### Highbury: The Stadium of Light

Arsenal Stadium, Highbury, was home to the club for **93 years**. Built in north London on the site of a former college of St John, it became one of English football's most architecturally remarkable grounds — with the **Art Deco East and West Stands** (completed in 1936 and 1932 respectively) listed as Grade II structures that still stand today, converted into luxury apartments.

At its final capacity of approximately **38,000**, Highbury was intimate, atmospheric, and deeply associated with Arsenal's identity. The famous marble halls, the cannon crest embedded in the main entrance, and the Clock End terrace are etched into the memory of every supporter who visited.

---

## Double-Winning Decades

The post-war era brought renewed success under manager **Tom Whittaker**, with the First Division title in 1947/48 and 1952/53. But it was the appointment of **Bertie Mee** in 1966 that catalysed the next great chapter.

Under Mee, Arsenal achieved the **First Division and FA Cup Double in 1970/71** — completing it with a last-gasp Ray Kennedy goal at White Hart Lane (Spurs' ground) to clinch the title, then beating Liverpool 2–1 in extra time in the FA Cup final at Wembley. Football at its most dramatic.

---

## The George Graham Era — 1986 to 1995

Manager **George Graham** built two successive title-winning teams using defensive solidity and lethal counter-attacking football.

Most famously: **26 May 1989**. Anfield. Arsenal needed to beat Liverpool by two goals to win the First Division on goal difference. Michael Thomas scored in the 91st minute to make it 2–0. Arsenal were champions. One of the greatest moments in football history — immortalised in Nick Hornby's *Fever Pitch*.

Graham's Arsenal won:
- First Division titles: 1988/89 and 1990/91
- FA Cup and League Cup double: 1992/93
- European Cup Winners' Cup: 1993/94

---

## The Wenger Revolution

The hiring of **Arsène Wenger** in September 1996 changed not just Arsenal, but English football. His impact on Highbury's final decade was extraordinary. See the [Wenger Dynasty](/history/wenger-dynasty) page for the full account.

Arsenal left Highbury in **May 2006** after a last league match that ended with a Thierry Henry hat-trick and tears from players and staff. The ground lives on — converted, preserved, irreplaceable.

<Note>
  Highbury's capacity constraints are a primary reason Arsenal pursued and built the Emirates Stadium, which opened in July 2006. The move tripled matchday revenue and enabled the modern commercial Arsenal we see today.
</Note>
