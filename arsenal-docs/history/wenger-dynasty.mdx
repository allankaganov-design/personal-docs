## The Wenger Era — 1996 to 2018

### Arrival of a Revolutionary

When Arsène Wenger arrived at Arsenal in September 1996, he was unknown to English football. The press was sceptical. Within two years, he had won a league and cup Double and transformed the sport.

Wenger brought:

- **Sports science and nutrition** — Arsenal players stopped eating Mars bars and started following carefully managed diets. Recovery, sleep, and injury prevention became systematic.
- **Continental technical football** — possession-based, quick-passing, attacking play that contrasted sharply with English football's physical directness.
- **Global recruitment** — Players like Anelka, Overmars, Vieira, Henry, Pires, Bergkamp, and later Fabregas, van Persie, and Alexis Sánchez arrived from across Europe and South America.

---

## The Double and the Invincibles

Wenger's Arsenal won the First Division/Premier League title three times:

| Season | Result |
|---|---|
| 1997/98 | Premier League and FA Cup Double |
| 2001/02 | Premier League and FA Cup Double |
| 2003/04 | **The Invincibles** — 38 games unbeaten |

The **2003/04 Invincibles** season is the defining achievement of the modern Arsenal: 26 wins, 12 draws, zero losses. Thierry Henry, Patrick Vieira, Robert Pires, Freddie Ljungberg, Ashley Cole, Sol Campbell, and Jens Lehmann. A team that played football as it was meant to be played.

No Premier League side has gone unbeaten in a full season since.

---

## The Emirates Transition and the Lean Years

The move to the Emirates Stadium in 2006 came at a financial cost. Arsenal spent nine consecutive seasons without a trophy (2005/06 to 2013/14) as the club prioritised paying down the stadium debt over transfer investment.

Yet Wenger's teams remained attractive to watch. Jack Wilshere, Cesc Fàbregas, Robin van Persie, and Samir Nasri were products of or developed under his care. The 8–2 defeat at Old Trafford in 2011 remains a low point. The consecutive Champions League last-16 exits — particularly to Bayern Munich and Milan — became a source of frustration.

---

## FA Cup Revival

Wenger's final chapter brought FA Cup silver:

- **FA Cup 2013/14** — 3–2 over Hull City in dramatic fashion, ending the trophy drought
- **FA Cup 2014/15** — 4–0 over Aston Villa
- **FA Cup 2016/17** — 2–1 over Chelsea at Wembley

Wenger became the most successful FA Cup manager in history. It was a fitting final trophy, even if the Premier League and Champions League had slipped away.

### Legacy

Arsène Wenger managed Arsenal **1,235 times**. He left in May 2018 after 22 years — the longest-serving manager in top-flight English football history at a single club. The statue outside the Emirates Stadium, unveiled in 2011, depicts him with arms outstretched — a posture as recognisable in north London as any player's goal celebration.

<Note>
  Wenger's influence on Mikel Arteta's methods is significant. Arteta spent three years as a player under Wenger (2011–2016) and later trained under Pep Guardiola — two of the finest football managers in history. The current Arsenal reflect both.
</Note>
